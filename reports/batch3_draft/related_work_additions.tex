\paragraph{Embodied VLA models.}
Vision-language-action models such as RT-2~\cite{brohan2023rt2}, OpenVLA~\cite{kim2024openvla}, and $\pi_0$~\cite{black2024pi0} translate visual observations and language instructions into robot actions. PIWM differs from this line in both action semantics and evaluation target: its action space is communicative and service-oriented (\textsc{Greet}, \textsc{Elicit}, \textsc{Inform}, \textsc{Recommend}, and \textsc{Hold}) rather than motor control, and success is measured by best intervention selection under customer-state constraints rather than physical task completion.

% Insertion position: Section 2.2, after the current proactive interaction paragraph.
\paragraph{Proactive multimodal assistants.}
ContextAgent~\cite{yang2026contextagent} explores proactive LLM agents with open-world sensory perception, while streaming-video and egocentric-assistance systems study when an assistant should speak or offer help. PIWM targets a narrower but more decision-critical setting: a third-party customer is observed from retail video, and the agent must compare candidate service interventions with explicit action-conditioned outcome supervision. This shifts the core question from whether to respond proactively to which low-intrusion service move should be selected.

% Insertion position: Section 2.4, after the current retail dataset paragraph.
\paragraph{Shopping and retail agents.}
WebShop~\cite{yao2022webshop} studies text-based shopping agents that navigate product pages, and Shopping MMLU~\cite{jin2024shoppingmmlu} evaluates LLMs on online shopping knowledge and reasoning tasks. PIWM instead operates on video observations of in-store customers and selects service interventions rather than navigating product catalogs or answering product-knowledge questions. The closest distinction is that PIWM models the customer's latent state and the expected effect of a service action before any explicit customer query is issued.
